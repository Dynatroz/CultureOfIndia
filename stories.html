Mountain Of Light

The Koh-i-Noor, meaning "Mountain of Light" in Persian, is a legendary diamond shrouded in history, controversy, and a touch of mystery. Unearthed in India centuries ago, its brilliance captivated emperors, sparking wars and conquests for possession. From the Mughals to the Persians, the Afghans to the Sikhs, each ruler coveted this magnificent gem. In 1850, after the British victory in the Anglo-Sikh Wars, the Koh-i-Noor was surrendered as a war prize. This marked a turning point in the diamond's journey. Recut to enhance its brilliance, it became part of the British Crown Jewels. However, the Koh-i-Noor's story didn't end there. India, Pakistan, Afghanistan, and Iran have all laid claim to the gem, demanding its return. The debate hinges on the ethics of colonial acquisition and the cultural significance of the Koh-i-Noor to India. Some believe its rightful place is back in its homeland, a symbol of its rich history and cultural heritage. Others argue it's a historical artifact best preserved in the British Crown Jewels. Today, the Koh-i-Noor continues to be a dazzling centerpiece, yet a reminder of a turbulent past. Its future remains uncertain, a gleaming testament to a bygone era and a spark of national pride for some, a weight of colonial history for others.

Maratha Lionheart

A fiery spirit ignited young, Bajirao I, the 7th Maratha Peshwa, ascended at 20. Earning the "Maratha Lion" title for unmatched courage, his military brilliance became legend. Lightning-fast cavalry charges became his signature, spearheading the Maratha expansion northwards, challenging the Mughal Empire. From Gujarat to Delhi's outskirts, his victories rattled their dominance. Beyond the battlefield, Bajirao wielded diplomacy like a weapon, forging alliances with Rajput kings to solidify Maratha control. Innovative guerilla tactics kept the mighty Mughals off balance. However, his love for Mastani, a Muslim dancer, defied societal norms, sparking a controversy that echoes to this day. Despite this, Bajirao's legacy remains. He transformed the Marathas from a regional power into a dominant force. His pioneering military tactics, emphasizing mobility and swift strikes, became a cornerstone of Maratha warfare. Though his reign tragically ended in his late thirties, Bajirao left behind a powerful empire and a reputation as one of India's most brilliant military minds. His story is a testament to courage, strategic brilliance, and the enduring spirit of rebellion.

Ink & Iron

A firebrand named Vinayak Damodar Savarkar, soon known as Veer Savarkar, ignited the Indian independence movement with his fiery words and unwavering spirit. Even as a young student, his nationalism burned bright. He penned articles and pamphlets, his words a weapon against British rule. In London, he co-founded Abhinav Bharat Society, a secret organization dedicated to overthrowing British dominance. This revolutionary zeal led to his arrest and a brutal sentence in the infamous Andaman cellular jail. Years of solitary confinement couldn't extinguish his passion. Here, amidst hardship, he developed his controversial vision for a free India – a Hindu nation. This concept, outlined in his writings like "Hindutva: Who is a Hindu?", continues to be a source of debate. While some revere him as a hero for his unwavering fight for an independent India, others find his ideology exclusionary and divisive. However, his dedication to freedom is undeniable. He endured solitary confinement and harsh treatment, his revolutionary spirit becoming a beacon for a generation of young Indians yearning for self-rule. Veer Savarkar's legacy is a complex one. He was a passionate advocate for an independent India, forever linked to the concept of Hindutva, a concept that continues to shape Indian politics and society even today.

Whispers of Melody

The veena, a captivating string instrument, boasts a history as rich as its melodies. Its origin story stretches back millennia, with some accounts placing it around 1500 BCE. Legend whispers of a hunter's bowstring, its twang inspiring the first stringed instrument. Early veenas were simple, crafted from nature's bounty – twisted bark and animal gut – yet their music resonated in temples and royal courts. Over time, the veena evolved into a family of regional stars. The Saraswati veena, with its long neck and intricate carvings, became synonymous with South India's Carnatic music. In contrast, the Rudra veena, larger with a prominent drone string, found its voice in North India's Hindustani music. The veena's magic lies in its design. Traditionally made from wood and adorned with beautiful carvings, its hollow chamber amplifies the vibrations of its strings. Unlike a guitar, the veena is played with a plectrum, a small pick that dances across frets, creating a mesmerizing range of notes. But the true magic lies in the hands of the musician. Skilled veena players bend and slide the strings, producing subtle nuances – a hallmark of Indian classical music. The veena transcends mere entertainment. It's a revered instrument, often associated with the goddess Saraswati, the patron of music and knowledge. Learning the veena is a demanding yet enriching pursuit.

The Battle of the Bowl

The origin story of Ras Malai, the king of Bengali sweets, is as sweet and debated as the dessert itself. The most widely accepted theory places its birth in early 20th-century Kolkata. K.C. Das Grandsons, a renowned sweet shop, claims their patriarch, Krishna Chandra Das, invented it in the 1930s. He supposedly adapted the already popular Roshogolla, a spongy cottage cheese ball in sugar syrup, by soaking it in a creamy, milk-based reduction flavored with saffron and cardamom. However, there's a challenger from across the border. The Sen brothers of Comilla district in Bangladesh (formerly East Bengal) claim a much older origin story. Their family business, Matri Bhandar, supposedly made a similar dessert called "Kheer Bhog" for centuries. This involved soaking Roshogolla in a thickened milk preparation. They believe the name and recipe were shortened and modified during the partition of India in 1947. Despite the debate, both Bengal and Bangladesh hold Ras Malai dear. The debate adds a layer of intrigue to this delightful dessert, which has become a symbol of creamy indulgence across South Asia. Whether a recent invention or an age-old tradition, Ras Malai's journey from humble beginnings to a celebrated sweet is a testament to the enduring love for rich and flavorful desserts in the region.
